The Guy

Coding to live, and continue living to continue coding. That's how my life is going and I'm genuenely proud of that: to study and have a related job that I love and that I think has a potential to improvement such that I can influence it and maybe see unfold during my lifetime what I've carefully helped to develop.

I like doing my best and at the end of the day either see that I got the job done or, while trying, that I got substantialy closer to piece the problem together. That's the programmer's job after all: to solve problems and to solve them efficiently enough given the deadline.

Programming languages, libraries, frameworks and what not are nothing more than tools which choice should be influenced by your ultimate need, given it had been thoroughly avalued at the time. I like to point that out because of how overwhelmed I am seeing people defend technologies like kids used to their favorite Pokémon. Of course, I have my opinions and preferences, but knowing when to put them aside when taking a decision is dificult, even more so to figure out you should do that in the first place. I do my best to realize that.

I love learning interesting things, so here's the things I'm confident about knowing given the proportional amount of experience I gathered. Plus, why I invested my time on them and what knowledge I was trying boost by doing so:

  • Linux

    Gives me full control of my computer and all the software it runs, even if just as a matter of choice. There is an awful lot of things to be learnt just by configuring your system, even if from the ground up to the state you've alerady been using for so long.

    Encourage me to learn the structure of the operating system and how it works. In this process you end up learning concepts and characteristics of operating systems in general.

  • Python

    Great learning curve, quick to get something done at a fundamental level, abstracted, metaclasses, official bindings to many technologies, many cli apps written with it, better package manager than npm, less gibberish-y code compared to javascript, alternative to javascript, slow but not because of bad feature development curation (Ruby), stockholm syndrome (Javascript) nor excessive and strictly followed philosophy (Haskell), great amount of learning and troubleshooting resources, great availability on almost every platform, great detailing on feature proposes such that they can be used as study resources on programming languages and user experience/need/opinion, extensive standard library, some documentation standard, oficialy recommended formatting style, some level of acknowledgment of the importance of static typing (type hints), easier extensibility with C (not that I personally see an use for that, but that gets the attention of who does and would to make it open-source) and great editor LSP support for both editing and debugging.

  • Javascript (client-side)

    Best way to code websites, official bindings to many technologies, many cli apps written with it, many job possibilites on the parts of the industry with the most contact with the average software user, extremelly straight forward event-loop-based concurrency API, turns out HTML and CSS are (personally) the best and most mature purelly imperative ways of representing an UI's stucture as code, turns out every desktop UI toolkit wants to have their own UI structuring language/method so I might as well stick to HTML and CSS because Electron general cost implication seems reasonable in comparison and one day a native UI toolkit based on an arbitrary language that uses HTML and CSS may appear and it may be actually good.

  • Keyboard-driven workflow

    I've grown to love convenience, specifically efficiency-driven convenience, like removing the need for a mouse in order do select and move stuff around my computer. Vim is the best example of this. It doesn't have in any way a straight forward learning curve, but the end result completely removes the mouse of the equation, and although I do not expect to maintain an over 100wpm typing speed during all my usage like the obssesive tryhards do, I have to admit that the comfort and delight of not having to reach for a mouse and slowly aim it at a button is completely worth it.

    Terminals, tyling window managers, vim-inspired web browsers (I'm not kidding), vim-motions-based file managers, vim motions extensions for other software, etc. I'm definitely still a newbie in using Vim, but I'm invested on it 100%, and not because its Vim (I tried Emacs, didn't like at all), but because It's just way more comfortable to reach everything using only the keyboard and even reducing the necessity to reach for modifier keys (ctrl, shift, alt or mod a.k.a. windows key).
